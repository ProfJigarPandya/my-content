<p><b>Kishore Kumar</b> (4 August 1929 – 13 October 1987) was an Indian film <a href="/wiki/Playback_singer" title="Playback singer">playback singer</a>, actor, lyricist, composer, producer, director, and screenwriter.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He is considered one of the most successful playback singers of the Hindi <a href="/wiki/Film_industry" title="Film industry">film industry</a>. Apart from Hindi, he sang in many Indian languages including <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">Marathi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">Assamese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_language" title="Gujarati language">Gujarati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kannada" title="Kannada">Kannada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhojpuri">Bhojpuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malayalam" title="Malayalam">Malayalam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odia_language" title="Odia language">Odia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a>. He has also sung in private albums in several languages especially in Bengali which are noted as all time classics. He won 8 <a href="/wiki/Filmfare_Award_for_Best_Male_Playback_Singer" title="Filmfare Award for Best Male Playback Singer">Filmfare Awards for Best Male Playback Singer</a> and holds the record for winning the most <a href="/wiki/Filmfare_Awards" title="Filmfare Awards">Filmfare Awards</a> in that category. He was awarded the "<a href="/wiki/Lata_Mangeshkar_Award" title="Lata Mangeshkar Award">Lata Mangeshkar Award</a>" by the <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a> government and from that year onwards, the Madhya Pradesh Government initiated a new award called the "Kishore Kumar Award" for contributions to <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">Hindi cinema</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kishore_Kumar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kishore Kumar was born in a <a href="/wiki/Bengali_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Bengali people">Bengali</a> <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> in <a href="/wiki/Khandwa" title="Khandwa">Khandwa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Provinces" title="Central Provinces">Central Provinces</a> (now in <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a>) as Abhas Kumar Ganguly.<sup id="cite_ref-KK_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KK-2">[2]</a></sup> His father Kunjalal Ganguly (Gangopadhyay) was a lawyer and his mother Gouri Devi came from a wealthy Bengali family. Kunjalal Gangopadhyaya was invited by the Kamavisadar Gokhale family of Khandwa to be their personal lawyer. Kishore was the youngest of four siblings, the other three were <a href="/wiki/Ashok_Kumar" title="Ashok Kumar">Ashok</a> (the eldest), Sati Devi, and <a href="/wiki/Anoop_Kumar" title="Anoop Kumar">Anoop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NabenduGhosh1995_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NabenduGhosh1995-3">[3]</a></sup> While Kishore was still a child, his brother Ashok became a Bollywood actor. Later, Anoop also ventured into cinema with Ashok's help. Spending time with his brothers, Kishore became interested in films and music. He became a fan of singer-actor <a href="/wiki/K._L._Saigal" title="K. L. Saigal">K. L. Saigal</a>—whom he considered his guru, and tried to emulate his singing style.<sup id="cite_ref-Bose2006_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bose2006-4">[4]</a></sup> He graduated from Christian College, Indore.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>After Ashok Kumar became a star of Hindi films, the Ganguly family visited Mumbai regularly. Abhas Kumar changed his name to Kishore and started his cinema career as a chorus singer at <a href="/wiki/Bombay_Talkies" title="Bombay Talkies">Bombay Talkies</a>, where his brother worked. Kumar's first film appearance was in <i>Shikari</i> (1946), in which his brother Ashok played the lead role. Music director Khemchand Prakash gave Kumar a chance to sing "Marne ki duayen kyon mangu" for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Ziddi_(1948_film)" title="Ziddi (1948 film)">Ziddi</a></i> (1948). After this, Kumar was offered many other assignments, but he was not very serious about a film career.<sup id="cite_ref-Filmfare_Nov1_1987_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmfare_Nov1_1987-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1949, he settled in Mumbai.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Kumar played the hero in the Bombay Talkies film <i>Andolan</i> (1951), directed by Phani Majumdar. Although he got some acting assignments with the help of his brother, he was more interested in becoming a singer. Ashok wanted Kumar to be an actor like him.<sup id="cite_ref-ToI_Avijit_unforgettable_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ToI_Avijit_unforgettable-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kumar next starred in <a href="/wiki/Bimal_Roy" title="Bimal Roy">Bimal Roy</a>'s <i>Naukri</i> (1954) and <a href="/wiki/Hrishikesh_Mukherjee" title="Hrishikesh Mukherjee">Hrishikesh Mukherjee</a>'s directorial debut <i><a href="/wiki/Musafir_(1957_film)" title="Musafir (1957 film)">Musafir</a></i> (1957). <a href="/wiki/Salil_Chowdhury" title="Salil Chowdhury">Salil Chowdhury</a>, the music director for <i>Naukri</i>, was initially dismissive of Kumar as a singer when he found that Kumar had no formal training in music.<sup id="cite_ref-raju_bharatan_repertoire_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-raju_bharatan_repertoire-8">[8]</a></sup> However, after hearing his voice, Chowdhury gave him the song <i>Chhota sa ghar hoga</i>, which was supposed to be sung by <a href="/wiki/Hemant_Kumar" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemant Kumar">Hemant Kumar</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Kumar starred in films <i>New Delhi</i> (1957), <i><a href="/wiki/Aasha_(1957_film)" title="Aasha (1957 film)">Aasha</a></i> (1957), <i><a href="/wiki/Chalti_Ka_Naam_Gaadi" title="Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi">Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi</a></i> (1958), <i><a href="/wiki/Half_Ticket" title="Half Ticket">Half Ticket</a></i> (1962), <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ganga_Ki_Lehren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ganga Ki Lehren (page does not exist)">Ganga Ki Lehren</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Padosan" title="Padosan">Padosan</a></i> (1968). <i><a href="/wiki/Chalti_Ka_Naam_Gaadi" title="Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi">Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi</a></i> (1958), his home production, starred the three Ganguly brothers and <a href="/wiki/Madhubala" title="Madhubala">Madhubala</a>. Kumar played a car mechanic who has a romance with a city girl (Madhubala) with a subplot involving the brothers.<sup id="cite_ref-Derek2004_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Derek2004-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:29</sup></p>
<p>In the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Half_Ticket" title="Half Ticket">Half Ticket</a></i>, for one of the songs - Aake Seedhi Lagi Dil Pe - the music director <a href="/wiki/Salil_Chowdhary" class="mw-redirect" title="Salil Chowdhary">Salil Chowdhary</a> had a duet in mind and wanted Kishore Kumar and Lata Mangeshkar sing the song. However, since Lata Mangeshkar was not in town and Salil Chowdhury had to record that song before Lata Manageshkar could return Kishore Kumar solved the problem by singing for both the male and female versions of the song himself. The duet is actually for Pran and Kishore Kumar on the screen dressed as a woman. It just turned out to be fine as he did admirably well singing as both the male and female.</p>
<p>Music director S. D. Burman is credited with spotting Kumar's talent for singing. During the making of <i>Mashaal</i> (1950), Burman visited Ashok's house, where he heard Kumar imitating <a href="/wiki/K._L._Saigal" title="K. L. Saigal">K. L. Saigal</a>. He complimented him and told him that he should develop a style of his own, instead of copying Saigal.<sup id="cite_ref-ToI_Avijit_unforgettable_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ToI_Avijit_unforgettable-7">[7]</a></sup> Kumar eventually developed his own style of singing, which featured <a href="/wiki/Yodeling" title="Yodeling">yodeling</a>, which he had heard on the records of <a href="/wiki/Tex_Morton" title="Tex Morton">Tex Morton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Rodgers_(country_singer)" title="Jimmie Rodgers (country singer)">Jimmie Rodgers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Derek2004_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Derek2004-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:60</sup></p>
<p>Burman recorded in Kumar's voice for <a href="/wiki/Dev_Anand" title="Dev Anand">Dev Anand</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Munimji" title="Munimji">Munimji</a></i> (1954), <i>Taxi Driver</i> (1954), <i>House No. 44</i> (1955), <i>Funtoosh</i> (1956), <i><a href="/wiki/Nau_Do_Gyarah" title="Nau Do Gyarah">Nau Do Gyarah</a></i> (1957), <i><a href="/wiki/Paying_Guest" title="Paying Guest">Paying Guest</a></i> (1957), <i><a href="/wiki/Guide_(film)" title="Guide (film)">Guide</a></i> (1965), <i><a href="/wiki/Jewel_Thief" title="Jewel Thief">Jewel Thief</a></i> (1967), <i><a href="/wiki/Prem_Pujari" title="Prem Pujari">Prem Pujari</a></i> (1970), and <i><a href="/wiki/Tere_Mere_Sapne_(1971_film)" title="Tere Mere Sapne (1971 film)">Tere Mere Sapne</a></i> (1971). He also composed music for Kumar's home production <i><a href="/wiki/Chalti_Ka_Naam_Gaadi" title="Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi">Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi</a></i> (1958). Some of their songs were; "Maana Janaab Ne Pukara Nahin" from <i>Paying Guest</i>, "Hum Hain Rahi Pyar Ke" from <i>Nau Do Gyarah</i> (1957), "Ai Meri Topi Palat Ke Aa" from <i>Funtoosh</i>, and "Ek Ladki Bheegi Bhaagi Si" and "Haal Kaisa Hai Janaab Ka" from <i><a href="/wiki/Chalti_Ka_Naam_Gaadi" title="Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi">Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi</a></i> (1958).<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopaedia_of_hindi_cinema_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopaedia_of_hindi_cinema-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Asha_Bhosle" title="Asha Bhosle">Asha Bhosle</a> and Kumar performed duets composed by Burman including "Chhod Do Aanchal" from <i><a href="/wiki/Paying_Guest" title="Paying Guest">Paying Guest</a></i> (1957), "Ankhon Mein Kya Ji" from <i><a href="/wiki/Nau_Do_Gyarah" title="Nau Do Gyarah">Nau Do Gyarah</a></i> (1957), "Haal Kaisa Hai Janaab Ka" and "Paanch Rupaiya Baara Aana" from <i><a href="/wiki/Chalti_Ka_Naam_Gaadi" title="Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi">Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi</a></i> (1958) and "Arre Yaar Meri Tum Bhi Ho Gajab" from <i><a href="/wiki/Teen_Devian" title="Teen Devian">Teen Deviyan</a></i> (1965).<sup id="cite_ref-Anu2011_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anu2011-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Music director <a href="/wiki/C._Ramchandra" title="C. Ramchandra">C. Ramchandra</a> also recognized Kumar's talent as a singer.<sup id="cite_ref-raju_bharatan_repertoire_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-raju_bharatan_repertoire-8">[8]</a></sup> They collaborated on songs including "Eena Meena Deeka" from <i><a href="/wiki/Aasha_(1957_film)" title="Aasha (1957 film)">Aasha</a></i> (1957). Kishore Kumar's work includes "Nakhrewaali" from <i><a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a></i> (1956) by <a href="/wiki/Shankar_Jaikishan" title="Shankar Jaikishan">Shankar Jaikishan</a>, "C.A.T. Cat Maane Billi" and "Hum To Mohabbat Karega" from <i><a href="/wiki/Dilli_Ka_Thug" title="Dilli Ka Thug">Dilli Ka Thug</a></i> (1958) by <a href="/wiki/Ravi_(music_director)" title="Ravi (music director)">Ravi</a>, and "Chhedo Na Meri Zulfein" from <i><a href="/wiki/Ganga_Ki_Lahren" title="Ganga Ki Lahren">Ganga Ki Lahren</a></i> (1964) by <a href="/wiki/Chitragupta_(composer)" title="Chitragupta (composer)">Chitragupta</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Kumar produced, directed, acted in composed the music for <i>Jhumroo</i> (1961), and wrote the lyrics for the film's title song, "Main Hoon Jhumroo". Later, he produced and directed <i>Door Gagan Ki Chhaon Mein</i> (1964). He wrote the script and composed music for the film, which is about the relationship between a father (Kishore Kumar) and his deaf and mute son (played by his real-life son (<a href="/wiki/Amit_Kumar" title="Amit Kumar">Amit Kumar</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Shantanu2005_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shantanu2005-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:52</sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s, as an actor, Kishore Kumar built up a notoriety for coming late for the shootings or bunking them altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-Filmfare_Nov_1987_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmfare_Nov_1987-13">[13]</a></sup> His films flopped frequently and he landed in income tax trouble.<sup id="cite_ref-ToI_Avijit_unforgettable_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ToI_Avijit_unforgettable-7">[7]</a></sup> As a singer, his work in this period includes "Zaroorat Hai Zaroorat Hai" from <i>Manmauji</i> (1961), "Gaata Rahe Mera Dil" from <i><a href="/wiki/Guide_(film)" title="Guide (film)">Guide</a></i> (1965), and "Yeh Dil Na Hota Bechara" from <i><a href="/wiki/Jewel_Thief" title="Jewel Thief">Jewel Thief</a></i> (1967).</p>
<p>In the late 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Rahul_Dev_Burman" class="mw-redirect" title="Rahul Dev Burman">Rahul Dev Burman</a> worked with Kishore Kumar on the soundtrack of the film <i><a href="/wiki/Padosan" title="Padosan">Padosan</a></i> (1968), in which Kumar sang "Mere Saamne Wali Khidki Mein" and "Kehna Hai." <i>Padosan</i> was a comedy in which Kumar as a dramatist-musician, <a href="/wiki/Mehmood_Ali" class="mw-redirect" title="Mehmood Ali">Mehmood</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Carnatic_music" title="Carnatic music">Carnatic music</a> and dance teacher, and <a href="/wiki/Sunil_Dutt" title="Sunil Dutt">Sunil Dutt</a> as a simpleton named Bhola. Kumar's character was inspired by his uncle, Dhananjay Bannerjee, a classical singer.<sup id="cite_ref-Filmfare_Nov1_1987_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmfare_Nov1_1987-6">[6]</a></sup> The highlight of the film was a musical, comical duel between Kishore Kumar-Sunil Dutt and Mehmood: "Ek Chatur Nar Karke Singaar."</p>
<p>In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Shakti_Samanta" title="Shakti Samanta">Shakti Samanta</a> produced and directed <i><a href="/wiki/Aradhana_(1969_film)" title="Aradhana (1969 film)">Aradhana</a></i>. He sang three songs in the film; "Mere Sapnon Ki Rani", "Kora Kagaj Tha Ye Man Mera" and "Roop Tera Mastana". Shakti Samanta suggested that Kumar sing the other songs. When the film was released, Kumar's two songs established him as a leading Bollywood playback singer.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Kishore Kumar won his first <a href="/wiki/Filmfare_Best_Male_Playback_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Filmfare Best Male Playback Award">Filmfare award</a> for"Roop Tera Mastana".<sup id="cite_ref-Shantanu2005_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shantanu2005-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s_and_1980s">1970s and 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kishore_Kumar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1970s and 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>S. D. Burman and Kumar continued to work together, including "Phoolon Ke Rang Se" and "Shokhiyon Mein Ghola Jaaye" from <i><a href="/wiki/Prem_Pujari" title="Prem Pujari">Prem Pujari</a></i> (1969), "Aaj Madhosh Hua Jaaye Re," "Khilte Hain Gul Yahan" and "O Meri Sharmilee" from <i><a href="/wiki/Sharmilee" class="mw-redirect" title="Sharmilee">Sharmilee</a></i> (1971), "Meet na mila" from <i><a href="/wiki/Abhimaan_(1973_film)" title="Abhimaan (1973 film)">Abhimaan</a></i> (1973), and "Jeevan ki Bagiya mehkegi" from <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tere_Mere_Sapne_(1974_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tere Mere Sapne (1974 film) (page does not exist)">Tere Mere Sapne</a></i> (1974). In 1975, S. D. Burman composed his last song for Kumar; "Badi Sooni Sooni Hai" for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Mili_(1975_film)" title="Mili (1975 film)">Mili</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>R.D. Burman recorded several songs with Kumar in the 1970s, including "O Maajhi Re" from <i><a href="/wiki/Khushboo_(1975_film)" title="Khushboo (1975 film)">Khushboo</a></i>, "Yeh Shaam Mastaani" and "Yeh Jo Mohabbat Hai" from <i><a href="/wiki/Kati_Patang" title="Kati Patang">Kati Patang</a></i> (1971), "Raat Kali Ek Khwab Mein Aayi" from <i><a href="/wiki/Buddha_Mil_Gaya_(film)" title="Buddha Mil Gaya (film)">Buddha Mil Gaya</a></i> (1971) and "Chingari Koi Bhadke (<a href="/wiki/Amar_Prem" title="Amar Prem">Amar Prem</a>)" and "Jab Bhi Koi Kangana" from <i><a href="/wiki/Shaukeen" title="Shaukeen">Shaukeen</a></i> (1986). Although he was not formally trained in the classical music, R.D. Burman often had Kumar sing semi-classical songs, such as "Hamein Tum Se Pyaar Kitna" from <i><a href="/wiki/Kudrat" title="Kudrat">Kudrat</a></i> and "Mere Naina Saawan Bhadon" from <i><a href="/wiki/Mehbooba" title="Mehbooba">Mehbooba</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Burman recorded several duets pairing Kumar with <a href="/wiki/Asha_Bhosle" title="Asha Bhosle">Asha Bhosle</a> and with <a href="/wiki/Lata_Mangeshkar" title="Lata Mangeshkar">Lata Mangeshkar</a>, including "Panna Ki Tamanna" from <i><a href="/wiki/Heera_Panna" title="Heera Panna">Heera Panna</a></i> (1973), "Neend Chura Ke Raaton Mein" from the film <i><a href="/wiki/Shareef_Budmaash" title="Shareef Budmaash">Shareef Budmaash</a></i>, "Rimjhim Gire Sawan" from <a href="/wiki/Manzil_(1979_film)" title="Manzil (1979 film)">Manzil</a>, "Kya Yehi Pyaar Hai" from <a href="/wiki/Sanjay_Dutt" title="Sanjay Dutt">Sanjay Dutt</a>'s debut film <i><a href="/wiki/Rocky_(1981_film)" title="Rocky (1981 film)">Rocky</a></i> (1981), "Jaane Ja Dhoondta" and "Kharoshoo" from <i>Harjai</i> (1982).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. L-P also worked with Kumar and <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_Rafi" title="Mohammed Rafi">Mohammed Rafi</a> on duets for the films <i><a href="/wiki/Dostana_(1980_film)" title="Dostana (1980 film)">Dostana</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ram_Balram" title="Ram Balram">Ram Balram</a></i> and <i>Deedaar-E-Yaar</i>. L-P composed "I love you (Kaate Nahin Katate Yeh Din Yeh Raat)" from <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._India" class="mw-redirect" title="Mr. India">Mr. India</a></i> in (1987), a duet with Kumar and <a href="/wiki/Alisha_Chinoy" class="mw-redirect" title="Alisha Chinoy">Alisha Chinoy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Salil_Chowdhury" title="Salil Chowdhury">Salil Chowdhury</a> recorded songs like "Koi Hota Jisko Apna" from <i><a href="/wiki/Mere_Apne" title="Mere Apne">Mere Apne</a></i> and "Gujar Gaye Din Din" from <i><a href="/wiki/Annadata_(1972_film)" title="Annadata (1972 film)">Annadata</a></i>. Ravindra Jain recorded "Ghungroo Ki Tarah" and the duets "Le Jaayenge Le Jaayenge" from <i><a href="/wiki/Chor_Machaye_Shor" title="Chor Machaye Shor">Chor Machaye Shor</a></i> and "Tota Maina Ki Kahani" from <i><a href="/wiki/Fakira_(film)" title="Fakira (film)">Fakira</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>During the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_in_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergency in India">Indian Emergency (1975–1977)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanjay_Gandhi" title="Sanjay Gandhi">Sanjay Gandhi</a> asked Kumar to sing for an <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress" title="Indian National Congress">Indian National Congress</a> rally in <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, but he refused.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As a result, Information and broadcasting minister <a href="/wiki/Vidya_Charan_Shukla" title="Vidya Charan Shukla">Vidya Charan Shukla</a> (1975–1977) put an unofficial ban on playing Kishore Kumar songs on state broadcasters <a href="/wiki/All_India_Radio" title="All India Radio">All India Radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doordarshan" title="Doordarshan">Doordarshan</a> from 4 May 1976 till the end of Emergency.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kishore_Kumar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kishore Kumar produced and directed some movies in the late 1970s and early 1980s, such as <i>Badhti Ka Naam Daadhi</i> (1978), <i>Zindagi</i> (1981) and <i>Door Wadiyon Mein Kahin</i> (1980)—his last appearance as an actor.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With patronage from R. D. Burman and Rajesh Roshan, Kumar's son <a href="/wiki/Amit_Kumar" title="Amit Kumar">Amit Kumar</a> became a Bollywood singer in the 1980s. Kumar continued singing for several actors and performed in stage shows to earn money to pay his income tax arrears.<sup id="cite_ref-Filmfare_Nov_1987_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmfare_Nov_1987-13">[13]</a></sup> Kumar stopped singing for <a href="/wiki/Amitabh_Bachchan" title="Amitabh Bachchan">Amitabh Bachchan</a> in the mid-1980s after Bachchan refused to appear as a guest in the film <i>Mamta Ki Chhaon Mein</i> which Kumar produced, but called a truce with Amitabh by singing for him in <i><a href="/wiki/Toofan_(1989_film)" title="Toofan (1989 film)">Toofan</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He also temporarily stopped singing for <a href="/wiki/Mithun_Chakraborty" title="Mithun Chakraborty">Mithun Chakraborty</a> after <a href="/wiki/Yogeeta_Bali" title="Yogeeta Bali">Yogeeta Bali</a> divorced him and married Chakraborty.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, Kumar sang for Chakraborty in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surakkhsha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Surakkhsha (page does not exist)">Surakkhsha</a></i> in the 1970s, and in the 1980s in many films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Disco_Dancer" title="Disco Dancer">Disco Dancer</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fareib&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fareib (page does not exist)">Fareib</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Waqt_Ki_Awaz" title="Waqt Ki Awaz">Waqt Ki Awaz</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1980s, Kumar sang for <a href="/wiki/Anil_Kapoor" title="Anil Kapoor">Anil Kapoor</a> in Kapoor's debut film as a leading man, <i><a href="/wiki/Woh_Saat_Din" title="Woh Saat Din">Woh Saat Din</a></i> and also recorded <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._India" class="mw-redirect" title="Mr. India">Mr. India</a></i>. He sang a duet with <a href="/wiki/Alka_Yagnik" title="Alka Yagnik">Alka Yagnik</a>, "Tumse Badhkar Duniya Mein Na Dekha" for <i><a href="/wiki/Kaamchor" title="Kaamchor">Kaamchor</a></i> in 1982 and recorded some songs for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Saagar_(1985_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saagar (1985 film)">Saagar</a></i> with R. D. Burman. By this time, Kumar had decided to retire and was planning to return to his birthplace Khandwa.<sup id="cite_ref-Filmfare_Nov_1987_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmfare_Nov_1987-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 October 1987—his brother Ashok's 76th birthday—he died of a heart attack in Mumbai at 4:45 pm. His body was taken to Khandwa for cremation. Kumar had recorded his last song, <i>Guru Guru</i>—a duet with <a href="/wiki/Asha_Bhosle" title="Asha Bhosle">Asha Bhosle</a> for the film <i>Waqt Ki Aawaz</i> (1988) composed by <a href="/wiki/Bappi_Lahiri" title="Bappi Lahiri">Bappi Lahiri</a> for Mithun Chakraborty and Sridevi—the day before he died.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His song <i>Pal Bhar Ke Liye</i> from the film <i><a href="/wiki/Johny_Mera_Naam" title="Johny Mera Naam">Johny Mera Naam</a></i> (1970) was used in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i> titled "<a href="/wiki/Kiss_Kiss,_Bang_Bangalore" title="Kiss Kiss, Bang Bangalore">Kiss Kiss, Bang Bangalore</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> His songs have been featured in several films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Such_a_Long_Journey_(film)" title="Such a Long Journey (film)">Such a Long Journey</a></i> (1998) and <i><a href="/wiki/Side_Streets_(1998_film)" title="Side Streets (1998 film)">Side Streets</a></i> (1998).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sony_TV" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony TV">Sony TV</a> organised the television singing contest <i><a href="/wiki/K_For_Kishore" class="mw-redirect" title="K For Kishore">K For Kishore</a></i> to search for a singer like Kishore Kumar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kishore_Kumar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kishore Kumar married four times. His first wife was <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a> singer and actress <a href="/wiki/Ruma_Guha_Thakurta" title="Ruma Guha Thakurta">Ruma Guha Thakurta</a> aka Ruma Ghosh. Their marriage lasted from 1950 to 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-Shantanu2005_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shantanu2005-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:53</sup> His second wife was actress <a href="/wiki/Madhubala" title="Madhubala">Madhubala</a>, who had worked with him in many films including his home production <i><a href="/wiki/Chalti_Ka_Naam_Gaadi" title="Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi">Chalti Ka Naam Gaadi</a></i> (1958) and Jhumroo (1961). When Kumar proposed to her, Madhubala was ill and was planning to go to London for treatment. She had a <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_septal_defect" title="Ventricular septal defect">ventricular septal defect</a> (hole in the heart), and he was still married to Ruma. After his divorce, the couple had a civil wedding in 1960 and Kishore Kumar <a href="/wiki/Converted_to_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Converted to Islam">converted to Islam</a> and reportedly changed his name to Karim Abdul.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> His parents refused to attend. The couple also had a Hindu ceremony to please Kumar's parents, but Madhubala was never truly accepted as his wife. Within a month of her wedding she moved back to her bungalow in Bandra because of tension in the Kumar household. They remained married but under great strain for the remainder of Madhubala's life. Their marriage ended with Madhubala's death on 23 February 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-Khatija1997_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khatija1997-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kumar's third marriage was to <a href="/wiki/Yogeeta_Bali" title="Yogeeta Bali">Yogeeta Bali</a>, and lasted from 1976 to 4 August 1978. Kishore was married to <a href="/wiki/Leena_Chandavarkar" title="Leena Chandavarkar">Leena Chandavarkar</a> from 1980 until his death. He had two sons, <a href="/wiki/Amit_Kumar" title="Amit Kumar">Amit Kumar</a> with Ruma, and Sumit Kumar with Leena Chandavarkar.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kumar is said to have been paranoid about not being paid.<sup id="cite_ref-Filmfare_Nov1_1987_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmfare_Nov1_1987-6">[6]</a></sup> During recordings, he would sing only after his secretary confirmed that the producer had made the payment.<sup id="cite_ref-tribuneindia_kuldip_prankster_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tribuneindia_kuldip_prankster-26">[26]</a></sup> On one occasion, when he discovered that his dues had not been fully paid, he appeared on set with makeup on only one side of his face. When the director questioned him, he replied "Aadha paisa to aadha make-up." (Half make-up for half payment).<sup id="cite_ref-Filmfare_Nov1_1987_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmfare_Nov1_1987-6">[6]</a></sup> On the sets of <i><a href="/wiki/Bhai-Bhai_(1956_film)" title="Bhai-Bhai (1956 film)">Bhai Bhai</a></i>, Kishore Kumar refused to act because the director M V Raman owed him <span style="white-space: nowrap">?</span> 5,000. Ashok Kumar persuaded him to do the scene but when the shooting started, Kumar walked across the floor, walked a few places and said, <i>Paanch Hazzar Rupaiya</i> (five thousand rupees) and did a somersault. After he reached the end of the floor, he left the studio.<sup id="cite_ref-kishore_definitive_bio_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kishore_definitive_bio-27">[27]</a></sup> On another occasion, when producer R.C. Talwar did not pay his dues in spite of repeated reminders, Kumar arrived at Talwar's residence and shouting "<i>Hey Talwar, de de mere aath hazaar</i>" ("Hey Talwar, give me my eight thousand") every morning until Talwar paid him.<sup id="cite_ref-tribuneindia_kuldip_prankster_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tribuneindia_kuldip_prankster-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film <i><a href="/wiki/Anand_(1971_film)" title="Anand (1971 film)">Anand</a></i> (1971) was originally supposed to star Kumar and <a href="/wiki/Mehmood_Ali" class="mw-redirect" title="Mehmood Ali">Mehmood Ali</a> in the lead.<sup id="cite_ref-hanif_mehmood_moods_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hanif_mehmood_moods-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hrishikesh_Mukherjee" title="Hrishikesh Mukherjee">Hrishikesh Mukherjee</a>, the director of the film, was asked to meet Kumar to discuss the project. However, when he went to Kumar's house he was driven away by the gatekeeper due to a misunderstanding. Kumar—himself a <a href="/wiki/Bengali_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Bengali people">Bengali</a>—had not been paid for a stage show organized by another Bengali man and had instructed his gatekeeper to drive away this "Bengali", if he ever visited the house. Consequently, Mehmood had to leave the film as well, and new actors (<a href="/wiki/Rajesh_Khanna" title="Rajesh Khanna">Rajesh Khanna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amitabh_Bachchan" title="Amitabh Bachchan">Amitabh Bachchan</a>) were signed up for the film.<sup id="cite_ref-hanif_mehmood_moods_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hanif_mehmood_moods-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In spite of his "no money, no work" principle, sometimes Kumar recorded for free even when the producers were willing to pay. Such films include those produced by <a href="/wiki/Rajesh_Khanna" title="Rajesh Khanna">Rajesh Khanna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danny_Denzongpa" title="Danny Denzongpa">Danny Denzongpa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-thehindu_kochi_yodeller_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thehindu_kochi_yodeller-29">[29]</a></sup> On one occasion, Kumar helped actor-turned-producer <a href="/wiki/Bipin_Gupta" title="Bipin Gupta">Bipin Gupta</a> by giving him <span style="white-space: nowrap">?</span> 20,000 for the film <i>Dal Mein Kala</i> (1964). When actor Arun Kumar Mukherjee—one of the first persons to appreciate Kishore's singing talent—died, Kumar regularly sent money to Mukherjee's family in Bhagalpur.<sup id="cite_ref-kishore_definitive_bio_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kishore_definitive_bio-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kishore_definitive_bio_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kishore_definitive_bio-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many journalists and writers have written about Kishore Kumar's seemingly eccentric behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> He placed a sign that said "Beware of Kishore" at the door of his <a href="/wiki/Warden_Road" class="mw-redirect" title="Warden Road">Warden Road</a> flat. Once, producer-director H. S. Rawail, who owed him some money, visited his flat to pay the dues. Kumar took the money and when Rawail offered to shake hands with him, Kumar reportedly put Rawail's hand in his mouth, bit it and asked "Didn’t you see the sign?". Rawail laughed off the incident and left quickly.<sup id="cite_ref-kishore_definitive_bio_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kishore_definitive_bio-27">[27]</a></sup> According to another reported incident, once Kumar was due to record a song for producer-director <a href="/wiki/G._P._Sippy" title="G. P. Sippy">G. P. Sippy</a>. As Sippy approached his bungalow, he saw Kumar going out in his car. Sippy asked Kumar to stop his car but Kumar increased his speed. Sippy chased him to <a href="/wiki/Madh_Island" title="Madh Island">Madh Island</a> where Kumar finally stopped his car near the ruined <a href="/wiki/Madh_Fort" title="Madh Fort">Madh Fort</a>. When Sippy questioned his strange behavior, Kumar refused to recognize or talk to him and threatened to call the police. The next morning, Kumar reported for the recording session. An angry Sippy questioned him about his behaviour the previous day but Kumar said that Sippy must have dreamt the incident and said that he was in Khandwa on the previous day.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once, a producer went to court to get a decree that Kumar must follow the director's orders. As a consequence, he obeyed the director to the letter. He refused to alight from his car until the director ordered him to do so. After filming a car scene in Mumbai, Kumar drove until he reached <a href="/wiki/Khandala" title="Khandala">Khandala</a> because the director forgot to say "Cut".<sup id="cite_ref-kishore_definitive_bio_27-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kishore_definitive_bio-27">[27]</a></sup> In the 1960s, a financier named Kalidas Batvabbal, who was disgusted with Kumar's alleged lack of cooperation during the shooting of <i><a href="/wiki/Half_Ticket" title="Half Ticket">Half Ticket</a></i>, reported to the income tax authorities, who raided his house. Later, Kumar invited Batvabbal to his home, asked him to enter a cupboard for a chat and locked him inside. He unlocked Batvabbal after two hours and told him, "Don’t ever come to my house again".<sup id="cite_ref-kishore_definitive_bio_27-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kishore_definitive_bio-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kishore Kumar was a loner; in an interview with <a href="/wiki/Pritish_Nandy" title="Pritish Nandy">Pritish Nandy</a> (1985) he said that he had no friends—he preferred talking to his trees instead.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Once, when a reporter made a comment about how lonely he must be, Kishore Kumar took her to his garden, named some of the trees there and introduced them to the reporter as his closest friends.<sup id="cite_ref-kishore_definitive_bio_27-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kishore_definitive_bio-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
